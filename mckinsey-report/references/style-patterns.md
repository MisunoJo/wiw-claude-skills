## Six opening moves

**A — Stakes statement, then a number:** "Governments and companies worldwide are pledging net-zero... we look at the economic transformation a transition would entail."
**B — Name a transition moment / paradox:** "If 2023 was the year the world discovered generative AI, 2024 is the year organizations truly began using it."
**C — Quantified shift, then the leader's choice:** "Nearly eight in ten companies report using gen AI — yet just as many report no significant bottom-line impact. We call this the 'gen AI paradox.'"
**D — Megatrend / "new normal":** "Families across the globe are having fewer children... Others may follow in the not-so-distant future."
**E — Imagine-frame** (use sparingly): "Imagine a world where machines not only perform physical labor but also think, learn, and make autonomous decisions."
**F — Picture-a-company scene** (feature articles only): "Picture a venerable industrial conglomerate where tradition has calcified into stagnation."

**Templates:**
- "If [year] was the year of X, [next year] is the year of Y."
- "Nearly [X] in [Y] [actors] report [behavior] — yet just as many report [no result]. We call this the '[label] paradox.'"
- "[Trend statement]. In much of the world, [supporting fact]. Others may follow in the not-so-distant future."

## Standard report architecture

Title (colon subtitle) → **"At a glance"** box (4-6 dense bullets, each with a number) → italic authors' line → opening section (no heading) → numbered chapters with sentence-fragment headings → declarative sub-section headings → sidebars cross-referenced inline → numbered exhibits cross-referenced inline → methodology / "About the research" → independence disclaimer.

**Length norms:** web article 1,500-3,500 words; feature 3,000-5,000; full MGI report 8,000-20,000+ (PDF 60-200+ pp); "At a glance" ~300 words.

For a solo report, the practical skeleton: title+subtitle → 1-page executive summary → 3-5 body sections (each led by an action title) → numbered recommendations → "About this research" (sources, dates, limitations).

## The finding-sentence formula

**[Subject of finding] + [conditional verb: could/would/may] + [quantified outcome, as a range] + [scope qualifier: annually / by 2050 / across N cases] + [comparison or methodological footing].**

Real examples:
- "Generative AI could add the equivalent of $2.6 trillion to $4.4 trillion annually across the 63 use cases we analyzed."
- "Across banking, the technology could deliver value equal to an additional $200 billion to $340 billion annually if fully implemented."
- "Fewer than 100 of the 8,300 large firms in our sample account for 63 percent of productivity growth in the three countries analyzed."

---

## Number rules (very consistent)

- **Ranges, not point estimates,** for any forecast ($2.6T-$4.4T, never "$3.5T").
- **"percent" spelled out** in body text; the % symbol only in charts.
- **Years spelled in full.**
- **Every big number anchored to a comparator:** "equivalent to the UK's entire GDP in 2021"; "half of global corporate profits."
- Both ends of a range share units.
- The audit-trail footnote appears constantly: "Figures may not sum to 100%, because of rounding." It signals someone checked the math.

## Hedging vocabulary (deliberate calibration, not weakness)

*could, may, might, would*; *estimates suggest, our analysis indicates, we find, our research suggests*; *all else being equal, absent action, on current trajectory, if implemented at scale.* The honesty footnote: "Our findings are not projections: we have taken an all-else-being-equal approach."

The voice is **authoritative but cautious** — confident on *direction*, precise-with-ranges on *magnitude*. Note this is the opposite of Wiw's usual "don't hedge everything" preference: in a McKinsey-style *forecast* the calibrated hedge is a feature, because it's honest about uncertainty. Stay direct and unhedged on recommendations and on things you actually know; reserve the conditional verbs for genuine forecasts and estimates.

## The confident-verb cluster

drive, accelerate, unlock, capture, deliver, reshape, reinvent, rewire, reimagine, redesign, harness, propel, scale, embed, navigate, double down, ramp up, rethink, rebalance.

## Handling nuance & counterpoints

Three recurring structures:
- **"but/yet" sentence:** "Gen AI can substantially increase labor productivity, but that will require investment to support workers as they shift activities."
- **"While X, Y" (concede, then re-assert):** "While generative AI is exciting and fast-advancing, the other applications of AI continue to account for the majority of overall potential value."
- **Explicit scenario framing:** "Our research is not a projection... It is the simulation of one hypothetical, relatively orderly pathway."

Typical closers: "Much depends on how the transition is managed." / "None of these are easy fixes, but in combination, they can…"

## Voice & register

- **First-person plural, always** — "we find," "our research suggests," "we call this." Never "I."
- Formal, declarative, neutral. No exclamation points, no rhetorical asides.
- Sentences ~24 words on average (range ~18-32). Paragraphs 3-5 sentences.
- Cadence: topic sentence → "for example" + quantified support → qualification → "as a result" / "this means" implication.

**Jargon they use** (house terms, fine to deploy): value at stake, step change, tipping point, secular shift, headwinds/tailwinds, productivity frontier, use case, archetype, road map, operating model, capture value, scale (verb), rewire, agentic, takers/shapers/makers, pace and scale, the next normal, "Done right… Done wrong…" closers.

**Jargon they avoid:** hype words (game-changing, revolutionary, mind-blowing, cutting-edge, world-class); motivational filler (synergy, leverage-as-noun, ninja, rockstar); conversational connectives (so basically, honestly, look). "Revolutionary" appears only inside attributed quotes, never in the authorial voice.

## Titling conventions

House standard: **two-part title with a colon** — evocative concept + descriptive subtitle.

Real titles:
- *The economic potential of generative AI: The next productivity frontier*
- *Seizing the agentic AI advantage: A CEO playbook to solve the gen AI paradox*
- *The net-zero transition: What it would cost, what it could bring*
- *The power of one: How standout firms grow national productivity*

Patterns: gerund-led main titles (Achieving, Seizing, Confronting, Reinventing); noun-phrase concepts (The power of one, The net-zero transition); parallel subtitles ("What it would cost, what it could bring"); the recurring "The next [X] frontier."

**Templates:**
- "[Gerund + abstract noun]: How [actors] can [verb] [outcome]"
- "The [adjective] [noun]: What it would [verb1], what it could [verb2]"
- "The state of [domain] in [year]: [short summary]"

## Anti-style — tells that scream "not McKinsey"

First-person singular ("I think"); personal-anecdote openings; chatty asides; hype adjectives in the authorial voice; exclamation points; emoji; unsupported claims; single-point estimates for forecasts; unstructured paragraphs; editorializing about the morality of a business decision; epistemic-shrug hedges ("we kind of think"); pop-culture references; treating one firm or person as the hero in institutional analysis; verb-tense drift (be precise: surveys in past tense, findings in present, scenarios in conditional).
