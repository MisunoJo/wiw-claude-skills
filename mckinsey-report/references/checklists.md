# Checklists — the pre-publish smell test, pitfalls, and reading list

Load this in Phase 5 (Quality check), or anytime Wiw asks "is this good enough?" / "what's wrong with this draft?"

## The 12-point smell test

Apply to every draft. Any "no" sends you back to the workflow step that fixes it.

1. Does every section title work as a complete sentence with a verb and a claim?
2. Can a reader find the answer to the key question in the first 100 words?
3. Have I tested every pair of branches for overlap, and the full set for gaps (MECE)?
4. Did I drop or thin at least 30% of my initial branches before deep research?
5. For every chart, can I state the "so what" in one sentence?
6. For every claim, can I point to a specific source or number?
7. Are charts free of 3-D, exploding pies, heavy gridlines, and decorative icons?
8. Does the voice stay consistent (third person plural, active, quantified) throughout?
9. Have I removed "may / could / potentially / we believe" *except* where uncertainty is the actual point (genuine forecasts)?
10. Does the executive summary end with explicit recommended actions?
11. Reading only the action titles in sequence — do they tell the full story?
12. Is there a methodology / "About this research" note naming sources, date ranges, and limitations?

**The three never-skip checks:** (11) titles-only readthrough, (6) trace three random numbers to their sources, (10) the exec summary ends with what to actually do.

## Common pitfalls → fixes

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---|---|
| Descriptive titles ("Revenue by region") | Rewrite as full-sentence findings |
| Buried lede | Governing thought in sentence 1 |
| Non-MECE structure | Test pairs for overlap; brainstorm gaps |
| Boil-the-ocean research | Re-prune; time-box each branch |
| Data dump | Write the "so what" first, then attach data |
| Insight with no data | Footnote every action title to a source |
| Chartjunk | Strip to data-ink; one accent color |
| Inconsistent voice | Global-find "I think", "kinda", "totally" |
| Weak verbs / over-hedging | Quantify the confidence or cut the hedge |
| No clear "so what" | End every section with a "therefore" sentence |
| Horizontal-logic failure | Print titles on one page; rewrite till they tell the story |
| Hidden methodology | Always include a 1-page "About this research" |

## Reading list — fastest path to competency (~25-30 hrs to ~80%)

Read in this order:

1. **Bulletproof Problem Solving** — Charles Conn & Robert McLean (2018/2019). The current public articulation of the 7-step method, 30 worked cases. The engine of this whole skill.
2. **The Pyramid Principle** — Barbara Minto (1978; current ed. 2009). Read selectively: SCQA, the pyramid rules, inductive vs deductive. Invented the firm's communication standard.
3. **Storytelling with Data** — Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic (2015). Modern, tool-friendly synthesis of Tufte + Zelazny: context, choose the visual, kill clutter, focus attention, design, tell a story.

Then, in priority order:
4. **The McKinsey Way** — Ethan Rasiel (1999). One-evening cultural orientation; popularized MECE and hypothesis-driven solving.
5. **The McKinsey Mind** — Rasiel & Friga (2001). Drills and exercises companion.
6. **Say It With Charts** — Gene Zelazny (1985; 4th ed. 2001). The chart-selection method (comparison type → chart form).
7. **The Visual Display of Quantitative Information** — Edward Tufte (2001). Data-ink ratio, chartjunk, the lie factor.
8. **Resonate** / **Slide:ology** — Nancy Duarte. Narrative arc and slide craft.

Free, authoritative:
9. **"How to master the seven-step problem-solving process"** — McKinsey podcast with Hugo Sarrazin & Charles Conn. The most concise free statement of the method.
10. **"Six problem-solving mindsets for very uncertain times"** — Conn & McLean, McKinsey Quarterly, Sept 2020.
11. **MGI methodology pages** — model the methodology appendices of the flagship reports.
12. **StrategyU (Paul Millerd)** and **Slideworks.io** — accessible online curricula and templates from ex-MBB consultants.
